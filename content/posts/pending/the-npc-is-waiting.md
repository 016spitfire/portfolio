A good idea doesn't have to be impressive to everyone. It just has to mean something to the right people. That's not a consolation — that's actually what makes an idea worth chasing. The best ones start small and personal and grow into something you didn't expect when you sat down.

Prism started with Game of Thrones.

Not the technical problem. Not the type system. Game of Thrones — specifically the way the show kept going after characters you thought were the story stopped being in it. Main characters died. The story didn't. The world kept moving, other people picked up threads, and slowly you realised the show wasn't about any one person. It was about a world, and you were moving through it one set of eyes at a time.

Two characters stuck with me. Podrick Payne — Tyrion's squire, comedy relief, the sort of character you'd expect to vanish between seasons. He didn't. He grew. He became someone. He was never the centerpiece, but he earned every scene he got, and by the end he was a character you'd have followed anywhere.

Then there's the knight Sansa saved from execution at Joffrey's nameday feast. A man with no real standing, given a fool's cap and told to be grateful. He spent seasons in the background, a running joke, practically invisible. Then the Purple Wedding happens, and this nobody — this background character — is the one who spirits Sansa out the door. He had one thing to do in this story and he did it. He was gone shortly after. But he mattered. He was important. He just didn't know it until the story needed him to be.

That's the idea behind Prism. Every NPC is a potential protagonist. The boy holding a sword line who kills a charging knight isn't a combat encounter — he's someone living a story you haven't seen yet. He has bad intelligence, shaking hands, a sergeant who wasn't where he promised to be. He stepped sideways instead of back, not out of training but out of panic, and now there's a knight dying at his feet who is old enough to be his father.

Transitions don't have to be deaths. A death is just the most final version of the same idea — this character's time as the protagonist is over, and someone else's begins. Prism tries to make that a design system rather than a scripted cutscene. The engine tracks who has shaped your story, who you've built bonds with, who the world considers important regardless of screen time. When the shift comes, it isn't random. It's weighted by the life you actually lived.

The library is a prototype. There's a lot left to build. But the idea — the reason it exists — is just that NPCs deserve better than the background. They're waiting for the story to need them. Prism is the tool that makes that possible.
