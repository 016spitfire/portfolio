---
title: If It's Too Simple, It's Not Worth It
date: 2026-07-09
excerpt: Miranor started as a Candy Land reskin for a college midterm. I ignored the brief and built a game instead. Nine years later I'm still building it.
---

My game design professor assigned a midterm project: build a Candy Land reskin, themed around something you like. Bonus points for adding a mechanic or two. Three weeks, a color card deck, pick a theme. That was the assignment.

I went home that night feeling deflated. Not because it was hard — because it wasn't. This was the one class in my two-year program I had been waiting for. Small cohort, individual attention, and I wanted to do something that actually mattered. Reskinning Candy Land wasn't it.

So I ignored the brief and built a game.

I started with a map. Used that trick where you dump something on paper and trace around it to get a random continent shape — I can't remember what I used, thumbtacks maybe, whatever the school had. I had my continent. Then I started plotting roads town to town and I thought: why does this have to be a single path? In real life there's more than one road between two places. So I added branches. Multiple routes, different towns, different bosses at the end of each path.

Then the gameplay. Four stats: strength, majek, dexterity, life. Strength doubled against majek, majek doubled against dexterity, dexterity doubled against strength — the same cycle in reverse dealt half. I cut cards out of drawing paper, five decks color-coded by level, each card either a monster or a piece of gear. I mathed out all the stats before writing a single card. The game was balanced on the first playthrough.

It also took twelve hours to finish. I was proud of that. My parents sat down with me and we played the whole thing — big map, essential oil bottles for meeples, stat sheets tracking equipped gear and experience. It played exactly the way I'd designed it to play.

The course went well. I graduated, got a job — not as the media designer I'd trained for, but as an app developer, which I knew nothing about. But as I learned, I could see how the tools I was picking up could change what Miranor was capable of. The hex grid replaced the hand-drawn map. Crit chance, crit damage, and search chance got added. The stat tracking moved into an app. Monsters got codes you'd type in to load their data and run the fight digitally.

That was when I started figuring out what the app and the game could be together. I didn't want to turn it into an online game — I wanted people sitting around a table. The app had to take what it could take and leave the rest to the players. Monster codes became QR codes. Party play meant scanning a code on someone else's phone to pass a monster between players. You could generate codes to sell items to other players — potions, weapons, gear — trading through your inventory by scan. It ran on the honor system. Anyone can cheat if they want to. You're only hurting your own playthrough. I made peace with that.

Rewrites happened when I learned something new or had a reason to take the game in a new direction. Crafting systems, town stores, looting, different types of shops. Each version was more capable than the last. The final push to a fully digital platform came when I moved to South Africa and had to think about production costs. Printing boards, cards, boxes, plastic molds — none of that is cheap or easy to ship. Pushing to a live web app is.

The game has never been simple. But that's not the point. The tasks were always meant to be simple — what had to be complex were the systems underneath them. A crafting system, a stat map, a combat engine — these things should be intricate. But using them shouldn't require a manual. You can have a skill tree that demands real thought and planning and still make it simple to interact with. Complexity lives in the design. Simplicity lives in the experience.

If I'd kept Miranor as a board game and tried to add everything it has now — the crafting, the monster system, the loot, the economy — you'd need a Uhaul van just to fit the boxes. The game would take over your living room and most of your weekend just to set up. That's not good design. That's complexity without discipline.

Each rewrite came from the goal of making the game more complex, or more accurately more *sophisticated*. Sophistication does two things — it either keeps or makes the gameplay simple for the players where it matters, but it allows your systems to grow more complex. If the game were too simple to build it wouldn't be worth it to make or for players to play and enjoy. A nine year old project deserves sophistication and complexity.
