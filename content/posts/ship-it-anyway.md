---
title: Ship It Anyway
date: 2026-07-07
excerpt: A half-answer in someone's hands is more useful than a perfect answer still sitting in your head.
---

My wife Steph knows plants. I do not. I know that some are green, some need water, and some apparently need to be left sitting in a tub of water to drink through the base — something called bottom watering that I had never heard of until I watched her do it. I was at my desk that afternoon thinking through ideas for new projects when she asked me something she'd asked before: did I remember when she last watered a few of her plants?

I did not. I never do. I am spectacularly useless when it comes to plants.

But this time something was different. I was already in the headspace of building things, and the question landed differently. I couldn't remember when she last watered her plants — but I could build something that could. That's how Gaia Plant Care started. Not from a gap in the market, not from a design brief. From sitting in the same room as someone I care about and finally having an answer to offer.

The problem arrived immediately: I don't know anything about plants. I could ask Steph what the app should track, but that would just give me her preferences. Every plant person is different. Some track fertilising schedules. Some track light exposure. Some track repotting dates. I might track if it seemed happier when I sang Imagine Dragons songs when nobody else is home. I had no way of knowing what the right fields were, so I did the only thing that made sense — I made them optional. A handful of sensible defaults and the ability to create your own fields for everything else.

It works. And it stretches well beyond plant care if you think about it — the same structure could track almost anything on a recurring schedule. But the honest tradeoff is that it puts work on the user. You have to decide what you're tracking before the app becomes useful. That's not a design principle I'm proud of. An app should be useful before the user has to earn it.

I'm not even sure this is actually the problem. I could add a few more fields, make it possible to hide the ones you don't plan to use — but honestly, the idea might already be finished and I just can't see it clearly. Something feels missing and I can't put my finger on what. It probably deserves to move out of alpha and into a v1.0, but something in me is holding it back because it feels like it should do more — and my lack of knowledge about plants is the blind spot I keep running into.

I shipped it anyway.

Not because it was finished — it isn't. Not because I'd solved the problem — I hadn't. But because a half-answer in someone's hands is more useful than a perfect answer still sitting in my head. The bones are right. The philosophy is right. The gap is that I still don't know enough about plants to know what else belongs in this system, and that's a gap I can close later.

There's a version of this where I wait. Where I research plant care properly, where I interview users, where I design a complete field set and eight more features before writing a single line of code. That version never ships. I've seen it happen — the project that stays in planning because the plan never feels complete enough to build.

Build the open version. Ship the incomplete thing. Mark it alpha, be honest about what it is, and go back to it when you know more. Progress is a better teacher than planning. The app exists. Steph uses it. That's already more than the version I was still thinking about would have done.
