title: Working Beyond the Constraint
date: 2026-07-04
excerpt: A companion app can only be as open as the game it accompanies. That felt like a ceiling at first — until it became the design challenge.
---

A companion app can only be as open as the game it accompanies. That's not a flaw — it's the definition. Diablo III has a fixed season structure: a journey with set chapters, an Altar of Rites with set nodes, conquests chosen from a fixed list, and a paragon system that runs on pure experience gain. There's no room to invent new objectives. The game decides what exists. The app decides how to present it.

That constraint felt like a ceiling at first. D3 Companion started as a simple tracker — click off tasks as you go, let the game handle the achievement side. Informational display, maybe. Which conquests are available this season, which class sets Haedrig's Gift was offering. Useful, but not different. Not worth sharing.

The first thing that made it worth sharing was the paragon goal system. I'd maintained a spreadsheet for years that did the same math — drop in an exp goal, divide it across 84 days, cross-reference against a separate paragon data sheet to find the corresponding level for each day. Practical, but tedious. The app replaced the tedium. Set a target, choose your milestones, get back a breakdown with the hard math already done. Each goal represents the same amount of experience, but the early ones take more work because your character is weaker. The daily target gets progressively easier as the season goes on — you're front-loading the grind, not spreading it evenly.

That was the first time the app did something I hadn't seen elsewhere. But the rest of it was still as rigid as the game itself or any other tool out there. Track your progress, display your data, stay in your lane. Not bad, but not mine.

The shift came from a single question: what might set this apart? The answer was more user input. Let the player define how they want to use the app. Let them plan their own path through the Altar of Rites. Let them choose which conquests they want to tackle and track those alongside the journey. Define their own way through the season — not the way the game expects, not the way I do it, but theirs.

It isn't my job to lead anyone through it by the nose. That's actually the opposite of what I wanted to do.

That said, I did want to offer my own path as an option — something personal to share with the community. The curated list is how I play: a blend of efficiency and personal preference, tasks ordered the way I actually approach the season. It's still a work in progress, and probably always will be. But it felt like a worthwhile thing to give back — not a guide, just a glimpse at how one player thinks about it.
