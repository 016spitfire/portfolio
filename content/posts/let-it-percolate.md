---
title: Let It Percolate
date: 2026-07-05
excerpt: A good idea can come from anywhere. The trick isn't finding inspiration — it's not smothering it the moment you spot a flaw.
---

As I was sitting up one night scrolling through an Ark Survival Evolved subreddit I ran across a post from someone who was grumpy that somewhere along the line they had lost a piece of equipment and was proposing a way to get it back. They wanted to go to a previous save, drop the item into a persisted save bucket called an obelisk, and go back to their current save and pull the item back out. Problem solved?

Not really. Not a problem solved — but what if that *did* work? What if that hack worked? Even better, what if that was an available mechanic and not a hack?

That thought stuck with me for a little while and I came up with a way to make it work. There were issues with it, but there was a developing story behind it. I'm not ready to get into the idea yet, but the point is that I didn't smother this spark of inspiration the moment I found a flaw with it.

That's how inspiration works. That's why "there are no dumb ideas" in a brainstorming session — not because every idea will come out a winner, but because there is always a glimmer of something to work with. How abstract is it to get a sliver of inspiration for a game mechanic from a Reddit post about someone with an incorrect idea about recovering something from a previous save? A good idea can come from anywhere, and any idea can be worked with enough to become that sliver of inspiration for something amazing.

A new guy started at the company I was working at about six months after I started. I came in knowing nothing — I was a graphic designer taking a hard left into programming. The other guy had gone through one of those code schools, much more formal training than what I had. I was nervous he was going to surpass me, but I noticed a pattern working with him. I would float ideas out there on ways we might solve different issues we were running into, and he would quickly respond with some variation of "there's no way to do that." Just a hard and immediate shutdown of any idea. That's fine — but why? Why won't that thing work?

Honestly, maybe he was right that it wouldn't work. I don't remember the issues we were trying to solve or how we solved them if we did. What I remember was the mindset, and that isn't a mindset to bring into creativity.

The difference isn't confidence or experience. It's the willingness to ask *why* instead of just saying "no." Why won't it work? What specifically makes it wrong? Does that thing make it a more interesting problem and point to a more interesting solution?

---

There's a moment in *He Who Fights with Monsters* by Travis Deverell that I keep coming back to. I'm paraphrasing from memory here, but the exchange goes something like this.

Jason, the main character, is early in his training. He has the knowledge of a combat technique from a skill book but not the mastery — so he's making it his own, filling the gaps with instinct. He spars with Rufus, his mentor, and deliberately leaves openings. He's baiting. Rufus eventually takes one and Jason gets the upper hand for a moment before Rufus shuts it down with full strength.

Rufus looks down at him and says:

> "You were baiting me, weren't you? Making mistakes so I'd attack where I thought you had an opening."

Jason replies:

> "Some of my mistakes were traps. I just had to wait for you to fall for the right one."

Rufus tells him he needs to close up the rest of the mistakes that weren't traps. Jason grins and says:

> "No. Now I need to make all of the rest of my mistakes into traps as well."

---

I'm not saying that all of the bugs in your code should be repainted as features. What this means is that a flaw in an idea isn't a reason to discard it — it may end up being the strongest part of the idea or the piece that gives it the most character. The concept I pulled from that Reddit thread had a significant problem with it that could break the economy of whatever story or game it was introduced to. I could have patched around the issue or dropped it entirely, but I didn't like that. I sat it down for a few days and let it percolate in the back of my mind while I was working on other things. Then I sat down with my wife one morning to have coffee and I brought up this idea, and we played with it a bit before she had to go to work. The solution we got to wasn't to remove the issue — it was to lean into it. Make that issue a part of the story. Not something to be ignored, but to be emphasized.

When you have an idea that just doesn't quite work, don't throw it away. Write it down. Put it somewhere you'll see it every day. Let it sit. Let your brain chew on it in the background for a couple of weeks and come back to it. Bring someone else in and discuss it with them. Work through *why* it won't work, then figure out how to make it work despite that issue.

The best ideas I've had didn't start amazing. They started flawed and unpolished. They were complaints, workarounds, questions that would have otherwise been ignored. I just happened to be paying attention when they showed up. Next time you have a thought for a project or an idea for something, don't dismiss it right away because you think it won't work — play with the idea until you have something you find genuinely enjoyable.
